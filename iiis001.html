"Data, Information & Knowledge"

The differences between data and information

- Data: raw facts
- Information: a collection of facts organised to have additional value beyond the facts themselves.

Example:

Data There are 20 PCs in-stock at the retail store.
Information The store will run out of inventory in a week unless more is ordered today.
Knowledge Call 800-555-2222 to order more inventory.

What it is?

Delivering relevant and reliable information to the right people to the right place at the right time.

Goal?

To achieve better decision faster.

How can I do that?

Requires methods & programs -> collect and structure data
--(convert)-> information

"Information Systems"

Information system is a set of interrelated components that collect, manipulate, and disseminate data and information, and provide feedback to meet an objective.

"TPS & MIS"

What is a transaction?

A transaction is any business-related exchange.

- Examples:
+ Credit sale to a customer
+ Lender borrowing
+ Investments
+ Cash transactions
+ etcetera

Transaction Processing System (TPS)

- A transaction processing system is an organised collection of people, procedures, data-based and devices used to record completed business transactions.
- Used at operational level.
- Goals:
+ To automate boring, repetitive tasks.
+ To extract data for processing, storeage and further use by other system.
+ Minimise errors.

Management Information System (MIS)

- A transaction processing system is an organised collection of people, procedures, data-based and devices used to provide routine information for managers and decision-makers.
- Management Information System typically provide standard reports generated with data and information from TPS.

Input Process Output
Transactions Scheduled reports
MIS Demanded reports
Other internal data Exception reports

MISs are used in all functional areas of the business!

"DSS & Expert System"

Decision Support System (DSS)

The focus of a DSS is on decision-making effectiveness. A DSS can provide immediate assistance in solving complex problems the were not supported by a traditional MIS. Allow managers and knowledge workers to consider alternative solutions to a problem via "what if" analysis.

Expert Systems

In addition to TPS, MIS, DSS, organisations ofeter use special systems such as artificial intelligence sytems.

- Mimic expertise and knowledge of experts rather than simply manipulate information.
- Matches user questions and answers to expertise in knowledge base.

- Medical diagnosis
- Help desk
- Financial planning
- Car diagnosis
- Credit card approval

"Business Process"

A business process is a standardised set of activities, that a accomplishses a specific task, such as processing a customer's orders. it includes manual procedures, work practice, and policies, executed to accomplish a business objective.

Some common business processes include:

- place an order
- recruit a staff
- hire the employees
- enrollment
- create timetable
- create invoice
- deliver the products

Functional business area:

Processes span functional business area in an organisation

"Business Process Improvement"

An example of the need for process improvement:

Customers waiting in line at a supermarket checkout to checkout the products with the cashier. Waiting in line at a supermarket is an example of the need for process improvement. In this case, the process is "checkout" and the purpose is to pay for and bag products.

The process begins when a customer steps into line and ends when customer receives the receipt and leave the store.

Examine the existing business process

Business process:

Customers waiting in line at a supermarket checkout the products with the cashier.

Examining the existing business process by:
- Determine bottleneck (The process takes too long to complete)
- Indentify the possoble errors.

To stay competitive, organisations must optimise and automate their business processes.

"A system analyst needs to understand what happens in a business operation, so that he/she can judge the effects of the processes to the business in order to achieve improvements in speed, quality, and customer satisfaction."

"Improved process" means "Improved business"

Business processes must help to satisfy the following business objectives:
- Achieve the result at minimum cost.
- Achieve the result at maximum effect.
- Avoid unnecessary errors.
- Add value to the organisation.
- be enjoyable or satisfying for employees or customers to complete.

"Swimlane Diagram"

Benefits of Business Process Improvement

- Improved efficiency
- Improved productivity
- Reduced operational costs

"Overview"

Goals

- Discuss several reasons to initiate a system development project.
- Out line how information systems are built traditionally with the System Development Life Cycle.

"Initiating system development"

Introduction

Have you ever wondered why businesses develop information systems in the first place, or how they know when it is the time to replace an old information system?

Systems development project may be initiated for a number of reasons. Can you think of some of those reasons?

Initiating System Development

System development projects maybe initiated for a number of reasons, including:
- problems with existing system
- increasing competition
- to make effective use of technologies
- organisational growth
- change in market or external environment

System development

System development is the activity of creating or modifying existing business systems.
Each year, billions of dollarsare spent on the acquisition of information systems.
Effective system development requires a team effort and it starts with careful planning.

"System Development Life Cycle (SDLC)"

SDLC is a traditional method used by many system analyst to help them in the development of a system.
- This methodology gives a step-by-step guide of what must be done in the process of developing a large system. The main idea of this is help system analyst to keep track of their progress, carefully planning and ensuring things are done in order.

The SDLC

Investigation

In this stage, potential problems and opportunities are identified.

Analysis

This stage involves the study of existing systems and work processes to identify strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities for improvement.

Design

The purpose of system design is to answer the question "How will the information system solve a problem?"
- Logical Design represents the logic nature of a system - what talks the system is doing.
- Physiscal design tells us where, how, and by whom a system's process are accomplished.

Implementation

System implementation consists of creating and acquiring the various system components and integrating the new or modified system into operation.

This phase includes:
- coding
- testing & debugging
- training
- hardware acquisition and installation
- site and data preparation
- documentaion

Maintenance

Maintenance involves checking, changing, modifying the system to make it more useful to achieve user and organisational goals.

"Participating in system development"

The development team usually consits of stakeholders, users, managers, special systems development specialist, and various other support personnel.

Stakehodlers individuals who will be affected by a systems development project.
Users individuals who interacts with the systems regularly, they are mostly the stakeholders of the information system as well.
-System analyst
-Business analyst		 a professional who specialise in analysing and designing business systems.
Programmer the individuals responsible for modifying or developing programs to satisfy user requirements.

The role of system analyst

System analyst play various roles while interacting with the stakeholders and users, management, vendors, and suppliers, external companies, software programmers, and other IT support personnel.

"Interview and Survey"

Interview

When do you need to interview:
- When you or the users are unsure of the requirements.
- Before you start designing the system throughout SDLC.
- To use responses to further design the survey questions.
- When the time is allowed and have limited numbers of respondents (usually 10->20 respondents)

Steps of a successful interview:

- Selecting appropriate sample and individual to interview.
- Preparing for the interview questions.
- Conducting the interview.
- Following up on the interview.
- Obtain the responses
- Double check the correctness of responses with the interviewees.
- Interpret the responses.

Questions types

Open-ended:

Allows respondents open options for the responding.
Example: "What are your positive experiences in using NetBank?", "Why do you use the Opal System?"

Close-ended

Limit the options the respondents has to answer the question.
Example: "How many times do you login to NetBank daily?"
The answer can only be 1time, 2-5 times, more than 5 times.

"User requirements"

Definition

User requirements are what the users expect the system would do for them. Since systems are created based on what users want, users plays an important role in system analysis by collaborating with system analyst to get the right user requirements.

Classifications of the user requirements

User requirements are classified to:

Essential The customers must be able to pay by credit card when using an online ordering system.
Desirable The customers should be able to join a membership to get discount when using an online ordering system.
Luxury Teh customers should be able to virtually try on the clothes when using an online ordering system.

Examples of poorly written user requirements

- The user may be able to view the customer record.
- The user might need to enter password to login to the system.
